Every successful company has passed through the following stages: startup, growth and consolidation.

Let’s see what these stages are about. Then, I will show you three reasons why it is important to walk again the same road whenever an established company wants to innovate with new products.

## Startup

In this stage the objective is basically one: find a favorable market position — market-fit — . This means, define the product that the market is willing to buy and that the company is in conditions to produce profitably.

It is necessary to experiment, learn and pivot fast so the product may find its right path.

## Growth

If the product has find its market-fit, the pains of growing appear. To increase the scale of what the company has been doing up to this point is a tough task. There are many internal dynamics that have been popping-up from the immediate necessities and not all of them can scale to a greater productive level in a satisfactory way.

Moreover, to grow also implies that you must start considering aspects of the business that were not a priority in the startup stage. Now, these new elements should grow and get integrated with the hectic pace of a business that barely survives while it grows.

## Consolidation

Here the product reaches a meaningful level of maturity and the internal dynamics of the business start settling. Job specialization is more evident now. It is easy to identify who is in charge of each operation and where are the limits of each team.

The job specialization in some places allow you to achieve deep knowledge in many complexities of the business. This is possible because the business can afford to invest time and resources in areas that are important but not urgent.

This road teaches so much, but also demands grit to be completed. Lets see then three justifications that consolidated companies make to avoid walking the whole road again when they are trying to innovate with new products. Lets check also why that route may not be the best.

## Three justifications to skip steps of the road and their potential costs

### It is not necessary to validate the market again

“We already have enough experience in this sector, so we can make a good guess about what works and what doesn’t. It is not necessary to invest money to test idea after idea until we find a market-fit.”

**Costs**: What we see here is that the company is deciding to jump the startup stage altogether. According with this stance, it is not necessary to connect with customers nor present ideas to test them. Possible costs of this may be:

The product finally doesn’t get sales and you learn the lesson after a lot of working hours invested. You learn late and costly.
The idea is not an idea built by the team, the idea is an instruction. The company skips the part where they have to defend the idea in front of a user or a potential investor as if the company’s survival depends on it. People’s hearts are not involved so you have at the end a heartless product that no one wants to buy.
To advance with the project without starting to sell as soon as possible is only possible because of the financial support an established company provides to the project. This comfort generates that this iniciative didn’t have to fight hard to sell the product. Those resources invested were used to delay facing reality. Again, you learn late and costly.

### We will do it how we operate our current products

> “Our current processes work, it is just a matter of hiring more people and replicate what we do now. In fact, we can do it with the people we already have, its just a matter of looking for more efficiency.”

**Costs**: Here the company is assuming that what worked once will work again, that you only have to check the details. The costs may be:

1. The operations become recipes and not the backup the product needs to be successful. You end up using what you have without evaluating and experimenting what is it that the product needs to prosper. The user experience (UX) at the end may be pretty poor and you see and feel that the processes are disconnected from the product in their roots.
2. The product evolution speed is drastically lower than the speed of the first product. A consolidated system may be an excessive burden for a newborn product, a system that is unable to adapt fast to the changes that the product demands. This way may lead to the dead of the idea because the lack of fast reaction from the company.
3. One thing is to look for efficiencies in a process that has a clear objective, another thing is to look for efficiencies so you can add a new objective that is a rival of the first. This may cause that the idea dies or that the consolidated business gets weakened. At the end, the business that creates the revenue usually wins and the new idea dies.

### Excess of complexity

> “After creating and supporting our current products, we are capable of understanding in a deeper level what we need to do to create a successful product. We can split the job into different parts and make each team tackle some of them. Like this we join the parts at the end and achieve a great product.”

**Costs**: Here the company is trying to use its full capacity and deep knowledge to create a great product, but is the engineering side of the problem the one that determines the product and not the user validated needs. The costs may be:

1. A significant number of highly qualified people enter the project and start creating partial deliverables of the product. This investment may result in a product that is highly competent in an engineering perspective but that the user doesn’t want or buy. This is just a huge waste of value.
2. Even if the company achieves a product that is desired and generates sales, maybe only 20% of their features add value to the client. A real startup wouldn’t have so many resources, so it would be necessary to align them to produce the features that added the most value. Here you have a lot of product features that are a waste of value.
3. When you support a product, such support is for the whole product. The problem here is that the product may have a greater degree of complexity than necessary. You pay the price with the slow pace at which you can create new value, because you have to be more careful to keep the product working with all the complexity involved.
4. A simpler user experience could increase customer fidelization. The competence can read what provides value and what doesn’t so they can create a rival product that sells even more and that is faster in terms of adapting it to the market. The company’s new product becomes obsolete soon.
   These are just three justifications that I’ve seen when a consolidated company decides to use when trying to innovate with new products, so they don’t have to walk the whole road of a startup again.

Yes, it is important to capitalize all the experience you gain over the years, it is not about starting from scratch.

What it is about is that you should walk the road again, execute the whole process with all your knowledge and capabilities, so you can now create high value products that no one else but you can create. At least not with ease.
