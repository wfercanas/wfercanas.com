This article is a reaction to the “8 Cognitive Biases in Software Development” article written by Matthieu in its webpage The Valuable Dev. I plan to make a quick brief of the key topics of that article, highlight certain positions and debate some others. Let’s go!

## Quick Brief

Matthieu points out several biases that are inherently present when we as humans are involved in making software (and I think this can be extended to whatever we intend to create, more so if it is a complex task). These biases are:

## The Optimisic Bias

Is the “tendency to be unrealistically optimistic about events happening around us”. In action it means we expect to solve challenges easily and internalize that assumption without checking the facts.

This tend to be more present when setting expectations about abstract and long term tasks.

## The Overconfidence Bias

Although not entirely developed in the article, we can get from it that is tightly related to the optimistic bias. In fact, that optimistic view in the future can be explained by the fact that we have pretty much confidence in our technical abilities. The overconfidence bias starts when even if we are proficient facing technical challenges, we think we are better than we actually are or our current abilities really don’t match the challenges to come — recall that part where we avoided checking the facts before stating an optimistic posture — .

## The Confirmation Bias

This bias highlights the tendency we have to only pay attention to the information that confirms our beliefs and pretty much ignore the ones which challenges them. Guess there is no need to add much, we love being right! And we give too much relevance to the facts that support our points of view and too little to the ones that don’t.

## Wishful Thinking

This one isn’t discussed much in the article, but I get that it is more a human trait that makes us hope for the best of the outcomes (optimistic again) with the best of all possible worlds happening while achieving such outcome. Guess optimistic bias is related to the soon and smooth realization of the outcome while wishful thinking adds to the situation the overlooking of risks in the process (!Murphy’s Law).

## The Anchoring Bias

The bias appears when our opinions are only based in the initial information we get, no matter if everything else proves us wrong. Particularly means that the capacity to question ourselves and what we “know” is not as powerful as we think it is. This makes us perpetuate behaviors, patterns and decisions even when all around us is evidence that we’re doing it all wrong.

## The Bandwagon Effect

We always will struggle when going against the current. When everybody is walking in a certain direction and we are not so sure about where to go, we will probably end up walking with them. To go against the current demands to think deep about the problem at hand, something that may not always be the case if we involve all the previous biases. Also, you assume that if they are walking in that direction is because they know better — more so if you don’t have a clue of where to go — , so you join them. Name they “senior” people, and you surely get the point.

## The Cargo Cult

Not so much to comment here. This one pushes us to follow blindly what the others do. Matthieu combines this one with the bandwagon effect so if you like, you can find more on this specific point here.

## The Correspondence Bias

This one is tough for humans as teamplayers. This pushes us to explain behaviors and results mainly based in personality traits or individual technical competence — or lack of it — without rightly involving the environment and specific situation that produced them. Is more clear when we are checking the work of someone else and we jump to conclusions linking the outcome to that person personality and competence, while when we or someone else checks our own work then we do decide to include all sorts of environmental and contextual information to judge the outcome.

Here I briefed the biases exposed in the article, but I didn’t mention the tactics it offered to deal with them. I recommend you to check them, because there are practical insights that can help you a lot in the future when creating software solutions or any other complex product, specially when doing it within a team.

Now, let me explain my position about the article through three ideas that I think can be valuable in this conversation.

## I Would Change the Article’s Name

If we analyze all those biases, they are all based in our human nature. That human nature is profoundly complex so we shouldn’t reduce the scope of the conversation solely to our cognitive dimension. That’s why I called this post “Human Biases in Software Development”, because to explore this field and to create solutions that makes us work better together and produce valuable results, we need to see ourselves as we are. My feeling while reading the article was that the article’s name is biased 😅.

## Let’s Stop Complaining About Meetings!

Please, please, let me say it again: let’s stop complaining about meetings! I know that jokingly Matthieu uses several example situations about meetings to place you in a known situation to clarify a point, but more jokingly is the fact that by checking the solutions he offers to keep in control our biases, most of them demand that we as humans collaborate to create new perspectives, to device processes that help us reflect on our assumptions and to commonly test them.

Phrases like “Bad News, you’re in the meeting room” or “You are in a feature meeting. Again. Don’t worry, it will be over soon.” I think don’t help. Yes, we must make big efforts to face our biases to create value, but we also must make big efforts to have valuable meetings, the ones that will enable teams to think deep, to not oversimplify things, to check facts, to manage risks, to empower each team member to speak up when everybody is walking in a direction he or she is not so sure its right or worth it.

Jokes are good to connect with the readers, more so in common situations that help you position your readers in a context that enlights the argument. Nonetheless, in this case I think the joke harms a key point in the discussion, which is that the nature of the problem is human-based and that its solutions are also human-based, we need to cooperate to keep in check our biases, join our perspectives and create better outcomes.

## Scientific Thinking and Speed

I fully agree with the debiasing methods offered by Matthieu to manage this human biases, even more when dealing with complex situations. So, in the line of thinking different and proposing new perspectives, I would like to add an embrace biases method splitted in two parts:

## There should be fun in facing them!

Solving problems that includes all these sort of challenges should be fun! More so if we are dealing with them as a team. There is only one cosmos — at least known by us — but there is a world for each living human. World in this context means all that each of us know and have experienced.

If we must collaborate to create valuable products, we must share our worlds. Enjoy knowing life through the complexities of your own world mixed with your teammates worlds, be willing to create a vision together and be willing to put it to test. Although is good to have methods to keep under control our biases, also enjoy the process of bringing to life a solution that had to face all the biases of all of the humans that made it possible.

## Set an hypothesis, test and learn fast

Just like the process of knowing each other’s world is full of endless stories, so is the process of creating software as teams. The vision you set may be full of biases but it shouldn’t matter as much as it is to put it to test. Create a story together and see if it can become a reality, test it rigorously, learn about it, device new ways of seeing your points of view in new perspectives and iterate again. Do it fast. Do so and you will create awesome products and enjoy life with all its complexities as well.
